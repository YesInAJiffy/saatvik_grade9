Entering a competition over the summer is a fantastic goal! Because many large, local competitions run on an academic school-calendar cycle (auditioning in the spring and wrapping up in June/July), the timeline for the next **2–3 months** falls into two categories:

1. **Immediate Summer Events (Deadlines in June):** Perfect if he has a piece performance-ready right now.
2. **Early Fall Deadlines (Deadlines in August/September):** Ideal if he wants to use the next 8–12 weeks of summer vacation to learn, polish, and record a brand-new piece.

Here are excellent, open-entry competitions for a 10-year-old with deadlines approaching in this timeframe:

---

### 1. American Protégé: International Vocal Competition

* **The Deadline:** **June 10**
* **Why it works:** Don't let the word "Vocal" fool you—this specific summer track is American Protégé's **Classical Aria, Musical Theatre, and Opera** branch, but it is highly popular for young independent musicians. If he enjoys playing lyrical, song-like classical pieces or musical theatre accompaniment/arrangements, this is an excellent fit.
* **Submission format:** Parents apply directly online with a YouTube link.
* **The Reward:** Winners perform at Carnegie Hall in New York City during the following concert season.

### 2. The Northern Lights Piano Festival & Competition (Summer Edition)

* **The Deadline:** Usually late **June or July** (Rolling summer deadlines)
* **Why it works:** This is an entirely online, international competition specifically designed to be highly encouraging for self-guided and young independent students. They judge students purely on their video submission, and they focus heavily on giving positive, constructive written feedback to help young musicians grow.
* **Submission format:** You upload a single unedited video of him playing a piece from memory.
* **The Reward:** Beautiful printable diplomas, digital medals, and a feature on their global winners' gallery.

### 3. "King's Peak" International Music Competition (Summer/Fall Flight)

* **The Deadline:** Late summer (They run multiple flights a year; the spring flight just closed, and the late-summer flight opens registration soon).
* **Why it works:** This competition is unique because the **initial round is completely free to enter**. You only submit a YouTube link of his performance. A panel of judges reviews it, and *only if* he is selected as a finalist do you pay an administrative fee to compete for 1st, 2nd, or 3rd place. This makes it completely risk-free to try out!
* **Submission format:** A YouTube video up to 30 minutes long (though for a 10-year-old, a 2- to 4-minute piece is perfect).

---

### The Perfect Alternative: The "Summer Intensive" Strategy

If he does not have a piece completely memorized and ready to film by early June, the absolute best strategy for his development is to treat **June, July, and August as his "Competition Prep" phase**.

You can use the summer to let him choose one of the pieces mentioned earlier (like the *Burgmüller Arabesque*), polish it to perfection, and hit the ground running when the massive local and international fall registrations open:

* **September 15:** Registration officially opens for the **Crescendo International Competition** (which holds local live auditions right in Massachusetts!).
* **October 1:** Registration officially opens for the **Boston International Music Competition (BIMC)** (our premier local open event).
* **October 20:** The deadline for the prestigious **American Protégé: Romantic Music Competition** (perfect for expressive pieces).

Taking the summer to perfect a video means he can enter 2 or 3 competitions all at once come autumn!

---

### 1. The Classical & Baroque Route (To show off his precision)

Since he already knows Petzold’s *Minuet in G* and Bach’s *Prelude*, he is ready for something with a bit more forward momentum that judges love.

* **Johann Sebastian Bach: *Musette in D Major, BWV Anh. 126***
* **Why it fits him:** It is bright, energetic, and joyous. It features a lively left-hand "drone" (like a bagpipe) and crisp, jumping right-hand melodies. It is short, easy to memorize, and highly competitive for a 10-year-old.


* **Christian Petzold: *Minuet in G Minor, BWV Anh. 115***
* **Why it fits him:** Since he already knows the famous *Minuet in G Major*, learning its companion piece in G Minor is a brilliant move. It uses similar finger patterns but has a soulful, dramatic, and haunting tone that shows a judge he can handle deep musical contrast.


* **Ludwig van Beethoven: *Sonatina in G Major, Anh. 5, No. 1 (Movement 1: Moderato)***
* **Why it fits him:** This is a classic competition piece. It has beautiful, flowing scales and dynamic shifts (loud and soft) that will show judges he can control the piano's volume perfectly.



### 2. The Romantic & "Character" Route (To show off his expression)

Judges love "character pieces" because they allow a child to act and tell a story through the keys.

* **Johann Friedrich Burgmüller: *Arabesque, Op. 100, No. 2***
* **Why it fits him:** Given how well he handles the fast, bouncy rhythm of *Super Mario*, he would absolutely ace this. It features lightning-fast, mysterious 5-finger patterns that move up and down the piano. It sounds incredibly flashy and impressive, but it is very pattern-based and rewarding to learn.


* **Robert Schumann: *The Wild Horseman (Wilder Reiter), Op. 68, No. 8***
* **Why it fits him:** This piece has a driving, staccato rhythm very similar to video game music. It’s short (around 1 minute), fast, and requires the melody to switch back and forth between the right and left hands—something judges look for to award high technical scores.



### 3. The 20th Century / Modern Route (To show off his rhythm)

Since he clearly loves modern syncopation (evident in his *Super Mario* video), playing a 20th-century classical piece will let him use those exact skills in a way that classical judges highly respect.

* **Dmitri Kabalevsky: *Clowns, Op. 39, No. 20***
* **Why it fits him:** This is a lively, quirky piece about circus clowns. It requires sudden jumps, sharp accents, and a sense of humor. His natural rhythmic drive from video game music makes this a perfect match.


* **William Gillock: *Splashing In The Rain* or *Fiesta***
* **Why it fits him:** Gillock writes modern pieces that sound like beautiful, cinematic film music. *Fiesta* has a brilliant Spanish rhythm with sharp chords, while *Splashing in the Rain* uses flowing arpeggios that make the piano sound like running water.
